# Executive Summary

This longitudinal analysis examines the evolutionary trajectory of **Knowledge Management** (*Gestión del Conocimiento*) spanning over seven decades (1950–2023). Conversely, both Google Books (876 data points, mean=10.4) and Crossref (888 data points, mean=11.93) demonstrate **increasing**, sustained momentum. The high standard deviations in the academic and literary spheres (16.28 and 17.56, respectively) indicate episodic yet significant spikes in scholarly attention, underscoring active and ongoing theoretical development.

This divergence illuminates a critical theory-practice dynamic. The decrease in web searches does not equate to organizational obsolescence; rather, it signifies the **institutionalization** of Knowledge Management. Practitioners are no longer searching for basic definitions or introductory frameworks—the tool has been normalized as a standard operational practice. Meanwhile, the academic community and management authors are actively refining the concept, likely integrating it with contemporary paradigms such as artificial intelligence, big data analytics, and organizational learning.

For corporate leadership, this trajectory dictates a necessary shift in strategic resource allocation. Organizations must move beyond viewing Knowledge Management as a standalone IT project or a transient consulting framework. Because academic research is continuously intensifying, new, sophisticated methodologies are constantly emerging. Companies that fail to upgrade their legacy knowledge systems risk experiencing severe bottlenecks in innovation, organizational agility, and intellectual property leverage.

Based on these empirical insights, executives are strongly advised to pivot their strategic approaches toward **advanced integration**. First, organizations should transition from static knowledge repositories to dynamic, AI-driven knowledge ecosystems that align with the cutting-edge research reflected in the Crossref data. Second, leadership should foster stronger ties with academic institutions to rapidly translate the latest scholarly insights into actionable corporate strategies. Finally, executives must embed knowledge-sharing metrics directly into core performance evaluations to reinforce the institutionalization process. By treating Knowledge Management as a continuously evolving, science-backed discipline, leaders can secure a durable competitive advantage in the modern knowledge economy.
