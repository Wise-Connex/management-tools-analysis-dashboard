# Executive Summary

This report synthesizes over seven decades of longitudinal data (1950–2023), encompassing 2,004 discrete data points extracted from Google Trends, Google Books, and Crossref. The aim is to evaluate the historical trajectory, current status, and future applicability of the strategic framework **Competencias Centrales** (Core Competencies). ### Key Patterns and Data Divergence
The most prominent finding is a significant divergence between real-time practitioner interest and formal academic discourse. **Google Trends** data (240 data points, mean=26.52, trend=**decreasing**) illustrates a waning popularity in active web searches by managers and practitioners. This contrasts sharply with bibliometric data: **Google Books** (876 data points, mean=14.63, trend=**increasing**) and **Crossref** (888 data points, mean=11.97, trend=**increasing**) both demonstrate a sustained, escalating interest in published literature and academic research. This asymmetry strongly suggests that while the terminology is no longer a trending buzzword, its conceptual integration into foundational business knowledge is deeper than ever.

### Theory-Practice Insights
This divergence provides a vital insight into the tool's lifecycle. Historically, seminal management frameworks experience a peak of curiosity-driven searches during their early adoption phase, followed by a decline. However, true strategic adoption is confirmed through academic canonization. The consistent upward trajectory in Crossref publications indicates that Core Competencies remain a highly fertile theoretical construct, continuously evolving to intersect with modern paradigms like dynamic capabilities and organizational ambidexterity. In the practical sphere, the concept has become so fundamentally ingrained in corporate lexicons and operational routines that executives no longer require introductory searches, focusing instead on complex, localized execution.

### Strategic Implications
For corporate leadership, the downward trend in search volume must not be misinterpreted as conceptual obsolescence. Rather, it signals a transition where defining core competencies is now a strategic baseline rather than a unique differentiator. The high standard deviations across datasets (14.48 for Trends, 18.5 for Crossref) highlight the high volatility and contextual variance in how these competencies are operationalized across different sectors. Firms that rely on static competency models risk falling victim to core rigidities, where historical strengths become blind spots in the face of disruptive innovation.

### Adoption Recommendations
To maintain competitive advantage, executives should pivot from basic competency identification to dynamic capability engineering. We recommend the following actions:
1. **From Identification to Architecture:** Organizations must map their existing competencies against emerging technological and ESG megatrends. Executives should focus on architecting new competencies rather than merely optimizing existing ones.
2. **Cultural Integration:** Given the tool's academic maturity, organizations should embed core competency development into continuous learning programs. Leadership must ensure that the workforce understands how daily operations feed into the broader, dynamic competency matrix of the firm.
