Conversely, both Google Trends (240 points; mean of 26.52) and Bain Usability scores (289 points; mean of 51.4) exhibit a clearly decreasing trend. This divergence points to a pronounced theory-practice gap. Since its popularization in the early 1990s, "Competencias Centrales" has become a deeply entrenched pillar in management literature, explaining the upward trajectory in Crossref data. However, the declining Bain and Google Trends metrics suggest that modern executives perceive the traditional framework as somewhat rigid, legacy-bound, or insufficient for today's hyper-competitive, digitally disrupted business environments. In practice, the tool is often critiqued for leading to structural inertia and "core rigidities" rather than fostering the dynamic capabilities required for modern agility.

### Strategic Implications
For senior leadership, the declining practitioner interest serves as a strategic warning: applying the traditional "Competencias Centrales" framework without critical adaptation may result in strategic myopia. Organizations operating in highly volatile markets risk anchoring themselves to historical competencies that no longer provide a competitive edge. Nevertheless, the academic resurgence suggests that the underlying premise—identifying and leveraging unique, collective organizational knowledge—remains fundamentally vital. The challenge for modern management is not the obsolescence of the concept itself, but rather its operationalization within fast-paced, agile contexts.

### Adoption Recommendations
To bridge this gap and restore the tool's practical utility, we recommend a recalibrated approach to adoption. Executives should pivot from static core competencies to building "Dynamic Core Competencies":
1. **Contextual Adaptation:** Integrate the framework directly with agile strategic planning cycles to ensure that organizational competencies are continuously re-evaluated against market shifts, preventing operational fossilization.
2. **Digital Integration:** Redefine what constitutes a core competency to heavily incorporate digital dexterity, data literacy, and algorithmic capabilities, moving decisively beyond traditional physical or rigid process-based strengths.
3. **Academic Translation:** Corporate strategy teams must actively monitor and translate ongoing academic advances (as reflected in the increasing Crossref data) into actionable internal frameworks, discarding the outdated models that are currently driving down usability scores.

In conclusion, while the orthodox application of Competencias Centrales is losing popular corporate momentum, its theoretical evolution offers a rich reservoir of strategic value.
