## Mean Analysis

The comprehensive mean analysis of the "Talento y Compromiso" (Talent and Commitment) management tool synthesizes 722 data points collected from January 2002 to December 2023. By triangulating public search behavior (Google Trends) with practitioner evaluations (Bain Usability and Satisfaction), a nuanced portrait of the tool's central tendencies and market positioning emerges.

**Public Interest vs. Practitioner Evaluation**

The most striking statistical revelation lies in the pronounced disparity between conceptual appeal and practical execution. The mean index for Google Trends stands at 41.84 (out of a maximum 100), representing aggregated search behavior over more than two decades. An average of this magnitude, particularly over a 22-year longitudinal span coupled with an increasing trend, indicates that "Talento y Compromiso" enjoys sustained cognitive resonance in the corporate ecosystem. The theoretical attractiveness of the framework remains highly relevant to managers, academics, and HR professionals.

Conversely, the Bain & Company metrics tell a markedly different story regarding practical application. The mean score for Bain Usability is merely 24.58, while Bain Satisfaction registers a similarly constrained mean of 28.14. When juxtaposed with the robust public interest mean (41.84), these low practitioner averages expose a critical *implementation gap*. The market is broadly interested in the philosophy of talent management, but when it comes to operationalizing the tool, executives encounter significant organizational friction and structural hurdles.

**Market Maturity and Strategic Positioning**

Analyzing these mean values through the lens of market maturity reveals a paradoxical phase in the tool's lifecycle. Typically, sustained high public interest suggests a concept residing at the peak of its maturity curve. However, the depressed means in usability and satisfaction contradict the hallmarks of a fully mature, seamlessly deployable management tool. Instead, "Talento y Compromiso" occupies a transitional space in the management landscape: it operates as a mature *strategic imperative* but functions as an immature *operational framework*.

The fact that the mean satisfaction score (28.14) marginally outpaces usability (24.58) suggests that when organizations do manage to navigate the implementation barriers, they derive a slightly higher degree of strategic value. This aligns with the observed increasing trend in satisfaction over time. Nevertheless, a mean score hovering in the high 20s unequivocally signals that the tool frequently fails to meet the lofty expectations generated by its high search visibility.

**Implications of Central Tendencies**

The central tendencies observed here strongly suggest that "Talento y Compromiso" acts more effectively as a guiding corporate philosophy than as a standardized management system. The high standard deviations across all three sources—19.7 for Trends, 25.57 for Usability, and 26.14 for Satisfaction—further emphasize the volatile, highly variable nature of its execution across different organizations. This mean analysis ultimately underscores that while the imperative to harness talent is universally recognized, the methodological architecture supporting its deployment requires a fundamental redesign to bridge the persistent gap between theoretical aspiration and executive utility.
