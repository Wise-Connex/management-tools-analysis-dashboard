# Executive Summary

This longitudinal analysis examines the evolutionary trajectory of the management tool "Propósito y Visión" (Purpose and Vision) over a 73-year period (1950–2023). By synthesizing a robust dataset of 1,128 distinct data points derived from both academic and practitioner sources, this report uncovers critical divergences in how the tool is consumed and operationalized across the theory-practice divide.

**Key Patterns and Data Insights**
A paradoxical trend emerges when comparing our two primary data streams. On one hand, academic literature—represented by 888 Crossref data points—demonstrates a clear, sustained increasing trend (mean=5.47, std=11.57). This indicates a growing scholarly interest in the theoretical underpinnings of organizational purpose. Conversely, practitioner and public interest, mapped by 240 Google Trends data points (mean=29.14, std=18.06), exhibits a pronounced decreasing trend. The high standard deviations in both datasets signal episodic volatility, suggesting that while researchers are steadily expanding the academic literature, executives are experiencing "tool fatigue" or shifting their focus toward more agile, operational methodologies.

**Theory-Practice Insights**
The divergence between Crossref's academic proliferation and Google Trends' practitioner decline highlights a significant translational gap. Historically, "Propósito y Visión" served as the foundational cornerstone of strategic planning. However, the data suggests that the traditional deployment of this tool has become disconnected from the fast-paced realities of modern management. While academia continues to champion its theoretical value, the corporate sector appears to be moving away from static, long-term vision-casting in favor of adaptable, short-term strategic pivots. This disconnect implies that the foundational frameworks of the tool are no longer resonating with practitioners in their classical, isolated format.

**Strategic Implications**
For organizational leaders, the declining search trajectory does not necessarily signal the death of purpose-driven strategy, but rather an urgent need for methodological revitalization. Organizations clinging to traditional, rigid visioning exercises risk alienating stakeholders who demand actionable, lived values rather than aspirational platitudes. The strategic imperative is to transition "Propósito y Visión" from a top-down compliance exercise into a dynamic, decentralized cultural anchor that drives real-time decision-making.

**Adoption and Implementation Recommendations**
To bridge the identified translational gap, executives must rethink the adoption of "Propósito y Visión" frameworks. We recommend a structural shift from isolated, periodic visioning workshops to continuous integration methodologies:
1. **Agile Purpose-Setting:** Organizations should treat purpose and vision as living documents, iteratively updated alongside quarterly strategic reviews rather than revisited on a multi-year cycle.
2. **Operational Integration:** Vision must be translated into specific, measurable KPIs at the departmental level, closing the gap between high-level theory and daily operational execution.
3. **Stakeholder Co-creation:** To combat tool fatigue, the formulation of purpose should involve cross-functional input, transforming it from an executive mandate into a shared organizational ethos.

In conclusion, while the intellectual capital surrounding "Propósito y Visión" remains strong within academia, its practical application requires immediate strategic recalibration to remain relevant and effective in the contemporary organizational landscape.
