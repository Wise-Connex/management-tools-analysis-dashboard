The three-factor structure identifies **three stakeholder constellations**: (1) academic knowledge builders, who dominate PC1 and define the theoretical narrative with 1,764 combined data points representing 69% of all observations; (2) the general public and media, represented by PC2, whose attention is volatile and declining; and (3) corporate practitioners, whose use-versus-satisfaction experience, captured in PC3, reveals the most critical gap. The academic community has been the primary architect of supply chain management's intellectual identity, while practitioners have been adopters who experience variable outcomes. For Supply Chain Management to maintain its strategic relevance in coming decades, it must address the usability-satisfaction tension, ensuring that academic sophistication translates into tangible organizational results. The declining public search interest need not be alarming —it may reflect normalization rather than irrelevance— but it does demand renewed efforts at communicating the discipline's value beyond specialized circles.

## Conclusions

The multi-source analysis of the management tool "Gestión de la Cadena de Suministro" (Supply Chain Management), encompassing 2,558 data points collected between 1950 and 2023, reveals a profound paradigm shift in how this discipline is conceptualized and applied in the modern corporate environment. The most significant finding of this longitudinal study is the stark dichotomy between mainstream popularity and deep operational and academic institutionalization. While general public interest has waned, specialized utility and intellectual capital generation have surged, indicating a definitive transition from a fleeting management buzzword to a mature, strategic necessity rigorously grounded in science.

**Optimal Timing for Intervention**
The empirical evidence suggests that the optimal timing for organizations to implement or overhaul their supply chain management systems is precisely the current era. We have entered a post-hype consolidation phase. Google Trends data (240 data points) exhibits a decreasing trajectory (mean=33.89), signaling that the era of superficial mainstream "buzz" surrounding the topic has plateaued. Conversely, the simultaneous upward trends in Google Books (876 data points) and Crossref academic citations (888 data points) demonstrate that the foundational science and literature surrounding this tool are richer than ever. Organizations no longer need to experiment blindly; they can leverage a vast, multidisciplinary, and highly mature body of research to implement evidence-based strategies.

**Risk Factors**
A critical risk factor emerges directly from the execution gap highlighted by the Bain datasets. Although Bain Satisfaction (mean=43.68) and Bain Usability (mean=36.34) show positive upward trends, their baseline means remain stubbornly moderate. Furthermore, the exceptionally high standard deviation in Bain Satisfaction (std=35.01) points to extreme polarization in corporate outcomes. This high variance indicates that while some firms achieve tremendous operational success, many others face severe disruptions and failures. Treating supply chain management as a mere technological installation rather than a holistic, cross-functional organizational transformation poses a massive risk of falling into the lower percentiles of the satisfaction distribution.

**Opportunities**
The uninterrupted, increasing trajectory in Crossref academic citations presents an unprecedented opportunity for direct knowledge transfer. Modern organizations have a unique window to bridge the persistent gap between academia and corporate practice. By translating the high volume of contemporary academic research into actionable operational protocols, firms can bypass costly trial-and-error phases. Additionally, the steady increase in usability scores suggests that emerging technological enablers—such as advanced analytics, the Internet of Things (IoT), and artificial intelligence—are finally making complex theoretical supply chain models practically executable on a daily basis.

**Implementation Strategy and Actionable Recommendations**
To mitigate inherent risks and capitalize on current opportunities, organizations must adopt a phased, academically-informed implementation strategy. Given the moderate usability score (36.34), initial rollout phases must prioritize user-centric design and operational simplicity over technological over-engineering.

1. **Pivot to Solid Value Creation:** Management must consciously ignore the decline in general Google Trends searches. Executive focus should instead be directed toward long-term operational resilience and consolidation of a data-driven continuous improvement culture.
2. **Address the Execution Gap:** Form dedicated, cross-functional task forces to investigate the root causes keeping overall satisfaction at a moderate 43.68. Ensuring seamless alignment between IT, operations, and procurement departments is imperative for success.
3. **Leverage Intellectual Capital:** Establish proactive collaborations with academic institutions to translate the massive surge in Crossref-indexed research directly into actionable corporate strategy, effectively leapfrogging competitors.
4. **Focus on Agility and Resilience:** Prioritize end-to-end supply chain visibility. Utilize modern technological integrations to push usability scores higher, ensuring that local disruptions do not cascade into systemic global failures.
