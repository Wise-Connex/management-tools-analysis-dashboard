Rather, it signifies a paradigm shift: CX has transitioned from a novel competitive differentiator to a fundamental, baseline expectation. Organizations continuing to treat CX as a standalone initiative rather than an integrated operational reality risk strategic obsolescence.

The widening gap between scholarly output and generalized search queries highlights a crucial theory-practice gap. Academic literature is successfully codifying the psychological and systemic elements of the customer journey. However, in practice, management is moving past the abstract jargon. The decreasing trend in Google Searches suggests that practitioners have passed the "peak of inflated expectations" and are firmly in the "plateau of productivity," focusing on execution-level technologies (such as AI-driven personalization or Voice of the Customer platforms) rather than the macro-concept itself.

**Adoption and Implementation Recommendations**
For executives considering the adoption or revitalization of CX strategies, this data provides clear directives:
1. **Operationalize the Abstraction:** Abandon generic CX branding in favor of specific, metric-driven operational goals. Focus resources on measurable touchpoints rather than abstract experiences.

3. **Integrate Foundational Practices:** Embed CX metrics into core KPIs across all departmental silos, recognizing that customer experience is now a systemic requirement rather than an isolated marketing function.

In conclusion, "Customer Experience" as a buzzword is contracting, but as an operational discipline, it is more critical than ever. Executive management must pivot from conceptual advocacy to rigorous, data-driven operationalization.
